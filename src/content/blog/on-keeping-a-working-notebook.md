I've been keeping a notebook for about a decade now — not the pristine kind with deckle edges and a ribbon, but the messy kind, the kind where the first page is a grocery list and the last page is a half-finished letter to nobody in particular.

The habit started, as most habits do, out of necessity. I was working on a project that had no clear brief, no clear client[^1], and no clear end. I needed somewhere to put the thinking so it would stop rattling around. A notebook, it turns out, is a remarkably good container for the rattling.

What I didn't know then, and what I'm still learning now, is that the *working* part is the whole point. Nulla facilisi. The page is not a trophy case. It's a workbench.

## Unfinished as a feature.

I think this is also why so many portfolios feel airless: they are trophy cases pretending to be workbenches. The work arrives *already finished*, already captioned, already reviewed. There's nothing to push against. Nothing to add to.

## A short grammar for honest captions.

If you keep a notebook long enough, a small grammar emerges. Mine, for whatever it's worth, looks like this:

1. **Say what it is.** One line, no adjectives.
2. **Say what it's for.** Who asked, what they wanted.
3. **Say what was hard.** This is the part people skip.
4. **Say what you'd do differently.** Even a year later.

That's it. Four lines. You can fit them under any figure, on any slide, in any case study, and you'll have told someone more than most portfolios tell them in a full page[^3].

### An example, in code.

Here's what this looks like in markup, for the developers in the audience. It's not complicated:

```js
// caption.astro
const caption = {
  what:      "A landing page for [PROJECT NAME].",
  for:       "Marketing, ahead of a Series B launch.",
  hard:      "Telling two audiences one story without splitting the page in half.",
  next_time: "I'd let the product speak earlier and save the narrative for below the fold.",
};
```

Four fields. Every project gets them. Over a year that's a record of what you actually did, and what it actually cost.

## What to do with the trail.

Keep it visible. That's really the whole lesson. The working part is where the `thinking` lives, and the thinking is what a client, a collaborator, or your future self is actually paying for. The finished thing is a receipt.
